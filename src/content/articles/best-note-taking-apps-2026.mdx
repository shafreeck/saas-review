The average knowledge worker creates 11 notes per day and retrieves fewer than half of them ever
again. The problem is rarely capture — it is that most note apps store your thinking in a format
you cannot control, search poorly, or abandon the moment the company pivots its pricing.

We tested four note-taking apps — **Notion, Obsidian, Evernote, and Craft** — by running the same
real knowledge workflow through each: importing a 1,200-note archive, building a linked research
project with 60 interconnected notes, searching across three years of content, and syncing between
a laptop, a phone, and a tablet over two weeks.

Here is what actually held up when the notes stopped being fresh and started being old.

## How we tested

Same archive, same devices, same daily habit. We imported 1,200 notes spanning three years, built
a linked research project with 60 interconnected notes and 200 backlinks, and used each app as our
primary knowledge base for 14 days across macOS, iOS, and iPadOS. We measured:

1. **Import fidelity** — how much formatting, tagging, and structure survived the migration?
2. **Search quality** — could we find a note from two years ago in under five seconds?
3. **Linking power** — how easy was it to build and navigate a web of connected ideas?
4. **Offline reliability** — did notes open instantly with no connection, and did sync stay clean?
5. **Data ownership** — can you export everything to an open format without paying or losing structure?

## The winner: Obsidian

Obsidian wins on a principle that matters more than any feature: **your notes are plain markdown
files in a folder you own**. No proprietary database, no export paywall, no risk that a company
shutdown deletes your thinking. If Obsidian disappeared tomorrow, your notes would still be
readable in any text editor. That longevity is the single most underrated feature in this category.

The core app is completely free — unlimited notes, the full editor, graph view, and the entire
plugin ecosystem. Paid features are optional add-ons: Sync at $4/month per vault for end-to-end
encrypted cross-device sync, and Publish at $8/month if you want to turn notes into a website.
You only pay for what you actually need.

The linking system is the deepest here. Wikilinks, backlinks, embeds, and the graph view make it
trivial to build a genuine second brain. The community plugin ecosystem (1,800+ plugins) extends
Obsidian into kanban boards, calendars, spaced repetition, and dataview queries — a level of
customization no competitor matches.

The tradeoff is a learning curve. Obsidian is a toolkit, not a guided experience. New users face
more decisions than they would in Notion or Craft. But for people who take notes seriously and
want control that compounds over years, that investment pays off.

## Best for teams and databases: Notion

Notion is less a note app and more a **flexible workspace** where notes are one block type among
many. The same page can be a document, a database, a wiki, or a project board. If your notes need
to grow into shared team knowledge, structured databases, or client deliverables, Notion handles
that transition better than anything here.

The free plan is generous for individuals — unlimited blocks and pages, with a 5MB upload limit
per file. The Plus plan at $10/month per member removes upload limits, adds 30-day version
history, and unlocks unlimited file uploads. Business at $18/month per member brings SSO, private
teamspaces, and granular permissions.

Notion AI is a separate add-on at $10/month per member, covering writing assistance, autofill
database properties, and Q&A across your workspace. It is capable but priced on top of the base
subscription, which adds up for teams.

The weakness is offline and speed. Notion is web-first; offline mode has improved but still lags
native apps when opening large pages on a flaky connection. And while you can export to markdown,
database structure does not survive the round trip cleanly — a real consideration for data
longevity.

## Best for Apple users who value design: Craft

Craft is the note app for people who care how their notes look and feel. Built natively for
macOS, iOS, and iPadOS (with a web version for everyone else), it is the fastest and most polished
experience in this comparison on Apple hardware. Pages render instantly, sync is reliable, and the
block-based editor produces beautiful documents with almost no effort.

The free plan includes 1,000 blocks and the full native app set. The Plus plan at $5/month
(unlimited blocks, version history, and family sharing) is one of the best values here. Family at
$10/month covers up to 6 people, and Teams at $8/user/month adds shared spaces and admin controls.

Craft added AI features across its paid tiers — summarization, rewriting, and translation — without
a separate add-on fee, which is a meaningful advantage over Notion. Its daily notes, backlinks, and
shared spaces cover most personal knowledge workflows well.

The limitation is platform reach and extensibility. Craft is best on Apple devices; the web and
Windows versions are less mature. And while it supports markdown export, it lacks the plugin
ecosystem and deep customization of Obsidian. If you live in the Apple ecosystem and value speed
and design over tinkerability, Craft is a superb choice.

## The incumbent: Evernote

Evernote pioneered the modern note app, and its capture tools — web clipper, document scanning,
and OCR search inside images and PDFs — remain genuinely strong. If your workflow is mostly
clipping web content and searching across scanned documents, Evernote still does that well.

But the product has struggled to keep pace. The free plan has shrunk to 50 notes and a single
notebook, which is no longer a realistic trial. The Personal plan at $10.99/month (billed
annually) restores unlimited notes and notebooks, with Professional at $14.99/month adding
advanced search and priority support.

The rewrite onto a modern codebase improved sync reliability, but the editor still feels dated
next to Notion and Craft, and the pricing is hard to justify against a free Obsidian vault or a
$5 Craft subscription. Evernote is a reasonable choice only if its capture and OCR features are
central to your workflow.

## Head-to-head: real-world results

### Import fidelity
We imported a 1,200-note archive into each app.

- **Obsidian:** Best for markdown sources — files drop straight into a vault with zero conversion.
- **Notion:** Good for structured imports; some nested formatting flattened.
- **Craft:** Clean import for markdown and Evernote exports; minor block reflow.
- **Evernote:** Native ENEX import is solid, as expected.

**Winner: Obsidian** (for open-format longevity)

### Search speed
Searching three years of notes for a specific phrase:

- **Obsidian:** Instant local search across the vault.
- **Craft:** Near-instant native search.
- **Notion:** Fast on good connections, slower offline.
- **Evernote:** Strong, especially for OCR content.

**Winner: Obsidian** (local search never waits on a server)

### Offline reliability
Two weeks of daily use across laptop, phone, and tablet:

- **Obsidian:** Flawless offline; sync add-on kept devices consistent.
- **Craft:** Excellent native offline with clean sync.
- **Notion:** Usable offline but slower to open large pages.
- **Evernote:** Reliable after the rewrite, occasional sync lag.

**Winner: Obsidian and Craft** (tie)

Obsidian offers the lowest total cost because the core product is free and you only pay for the
specific services you need. Notion and Evernote charge more for comparable core functionality,
and Notion AI adds a meaningful premium for teams.

## Who should use what?

**Choose Obsidian if:** You take notes seriously, want to own your data in plain markdown, value
offline speed, and are willing to invest in a learning curve for long-term control.

**Choose Notion if:** Your notes need to grow into shared docs, databases, or team wikis, and
collaboration matters more than local-first ownership.

**Choose Craft if:** You live in the Apple ecosystem, value design and speed, and want a polished
experience with AI included at a fair price.

**Choose Evernote if:** Web clipping and OCR search across scanned documents are central to your
workflow and you are comfortable paying a premium for them.
