The average person manages 168 passwords for work and personal accounts. The average person also
reuses the same 5 passwords across most of them. Password managers exist to close that gap — but
only if you actually trust the vault holding your keys.

We tested four password managers — **1Password, Bitwarden, Dashlane, and Proton Pass** — by
migrating a 400-credential vault into each, using them daily for two weeks across macOS, Windows,
iOS, Android, and three browser extensions, and stress-testing their sharing, breach alert, and
autofill features.

Here is what actually mattered when the password prompt appeared.

## How we tested

Same vault, same devices, same daily workflow. We imported 400 credentials (passwords, secure
notes, payment cards, identities) into each manager and used them as our primary password solution
for 14 days. We measured:

1. **Autofill reliability** — did it fill the right field on the first click across 50 test sites?
2. **Cross-platform sync** — how fast did a new credential appear on all five devices?
3. **Sharing workflow** — could we share a login with a family member or teammate in under 30 seconds?
4. **Security posture** — encryption standard, audit history, jurisdiction, and open-source status
5. **Breach monitoring** — did it alert us when a test credential appeared in a known breach?

## The winner: Bitwarden

Bitwarden wins on a principle that matters more than any feature list: **you can verify it works**.
The entire codebase — server, client, browser extension, mobile app — is open-source and has been
independently audited by Cure53, Insight Risk Consulting, and most recently in 2025. No other
password manager in this comparison offers that level of transparency.

The free tier is genuinely complete for individuals: unlimited passwords, unlimited devices (the
old 2-device limit was removed), TOTP authenticator, and end-to-end encryption with AES-256-CBC.
The Premium tier at $10/year (less than $1/month) adds 1GB encrypted file storage, advanced 2FA
options (YubiKey, FIDO2), and emergency access.

For teams, Bitwarden Organizations start at $3/user/month with shared collections, event logging,
and directory sync. The Enterprise tier at $5/user/month adds SSO, SCIM provisioning, and
self-hosting options.

The autofill engine is not as polished as 1Password — it occasionally requires a second click on
complex multi-field forms — but it works reliably on 47 of the 50 sites we tested. For the price
of a coffee per year, that tradeoff is easy to accept.

## Best for teams and families: 1Password

1Password is the password manager that makes security feel effortless. The interface is the most
refined in the category — every interaction, from saving a new login to sharing a vault with a
spouse, takes exactly the number of clicks you would expect. No more, no fewer.

At $2.99/month (billed annually) for individuals, you get unlimited passwords, unlimited devices,
1GB document storage, Travel Mode (which removes sensitive vaults from your device at border
crossings), and Watchtower breach monitoring. The Families plan at $4.99/month covers up to 5
people with shared vaults and permission controls.

The Teams plan at $7.99/user/month adds admin controls, activity logs, and 5GB per user. Business
at $19.95/user/month brings SCIM provisioning, advanced reporting, and a 99.9% uptime SLA.

The security architecture uses a Secret Key combined with your master password — even 1Password
cannot access your vault. The company has completed SOC 2 Type II certification and publishes its
security whitepaper publicly.

## Best for privacy purists: Proton Pass

Proton Pass is the newest entrant in this comparison, launched by the team behind Proton Mail and
Proton VPN. Its differentiator is jurisdiction and philosophy: servers are in Switzerland, the
code is open-source, and the company has built its reputation on resisting data requests.

The free tier includes unlimited passwords, unlimited devices, and 10 hide-my-email aliases —
unique addresses that forward to your real inbox, keeping your actual email off breach lists. The
Plus plan at $3.99/month (billed annually) unlocks unlimited aliases, 2FA integration, and
priority support.

The Proton Pass for Teams plan at $6.99/user/month adds shared vaults, admin controls, and
centralized billing. Proton also offers a bundled Proton Unlimited plan at $9.99/month that
includes Mail, VPN, Drive, and Pass together.

The autofill engine is the least mature of the four — it struggled with 8 of our 50 test sites,
mostly older forms with non-standard field naming. But for users who prioritize jurisdiction and
open-source verification over autofill perfection, Proton Pass is the strongest choice.

## The incumbent: Dashlane

Dashlane was once the default recommendation for password managers, and it still has features no
competitor matches: a built-in VPN (on premium plans), dark web monitoring that scans for your
personal data on breach marketplaces, and a password health score that gamifies security hygiene.

The free tier is the most limited here — 25 passwords on a single device. The Premium plan at
$4.99/month (billed annually) unlocks unlimited passwords, unlimited devices, the VPN, dark web
monitoring, and 1GB encrypted storage. The Friends & Family plan at $7.49/month covers up to 10
people.

For business, the Team plan at $4/user/month (billed annually) adds admin console and SSO. The
Business plan at $6/user/month brings SCIM, directory sync, and advanced reporting.

The problem is value. At $4.99/month, Dashlane costs 6x more than Bitwarden Premium ($10/year)
for a similar core password management experience. The VPN is a nice bonus, but it is not a
replacement for a dedicated VPN service, and the dark web monitoring, while useful, is available
standalone from services like Have I Been Pwned.

## Head-to-Head: Real-World Tests

### Test 1: Autofill reliability (50 sites)

We tested autofill on 50 real websites: banking, social media, e-commerce, government portals,
and legacy enterprise apps.

| Manager | First-click success | Needed retry | Failed |
|---------|-------------------|--------------|--------|
| 1Password | 49/50 | 1/50 | 0 |
| Bitwarden | 47/50 | 3/50 | 0 |
| Proton Pass | 42/50 | 6/50 | 2 |
| Dashlane | 48/50 | 2/50 | 0 |

**Winner: 1Password** — near-perfect autofill with zero failures.

### Test 2: Cross-device sync speed

We created a new credential on macOS and measured how long it appeared on iOS, Android, Windows,
and the browser extension.

| Manager | Avg. sync time | Notes |
|---------|---------------|-------|
| 1Password | < 2 seconds | Instant across all devices |
| Bitwarden | < 3 seconds | Occasional 5s delay on mobile |
| Dashlane | < 3 seconds | Consistent across platforms |
| Proton Pass | < 5 seconds | Slightly slower on first sync |

**Winner: 1Password** — but all four are fast enough for daily use.

### Test 3: Sharing a login with a family member

We timed how long it took to share a Netflix login with a spouse.

| Manager | Time | Steps |
|---------|------|-------|
| 1Password | 12 seconds | Open item → Share → Enter email → Send |
| Bitwarden | 25 seconds | Open item → Share → Select collection → Confirm |
| Dashlane | 18 seconds | Open item → Share → Enter email → Send |
| Proton Pass | 20 seconds | Open item → Share → Enter email → Send |

**Winner: 1Password** — the sharing flow is the most intuitive.

## Who Should Use What?

**Choose Bitwarden if:** You want open-source transparency, the best free tier, and the lowest
paid pricing. Ideal for individuals, developers, and budget-conscious teams.

**Choose 1Password if:** You want the most polished experience, need Travel Mode for international
travel, or are managing passwords for a family or team that values ease of use.

**Choose Proton Pass if:** You prioritize Swiss jurisdiction, want email aliases built in, or
already use the Proton ecosystem (Mail, VPN, Drive).

**Choose Dashlane if:** You want a password manager + VPN + dark web monitoring bundle and do not
mind paying a premium for convenience.
