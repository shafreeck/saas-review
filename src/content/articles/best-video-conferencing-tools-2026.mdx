Video conferencing stopped being a product category and became a utility — which means the
question is no longer "can we see each other?" but "how much friction does this tool add to
every single meeting?"

We tested four platforms — **Zoom Workplace, Google Meet, Microsoft Teams, and Webex** — by
running identical meeting scenarios: a 12-person all-hands with screen sharing, a 4-person
client call with recording, a webinar-style session with 100 attendees, and a low-bandwidth
call on hotel Wi-Fi.

Here is what actually separated them when the connection got rough.

## How we tested

Same meetings, same participants, same network conditions. We ran four scenarios across each
platform with a 12-person distributed team:

1. **Audio/video quality** — subjective scoring across good and degraded network conditions,
   plus objective measurements of latency and frame rate
2. **Screen sharing fidelity** — text readability, video playback smoothness, and annotation tools
3. **Recording and AI features** — transcript accuracy, summary quality, and action item extraction
4. **Guest friction** — how many clicks for an external participant to join without an account
5. **Pricing honesty** — what you actually pay for a functional 10-person setup

## The winner: Zoom Workplace

Zoom wins on the metric that matters most for a video tool: **it just works**. In our
low-bandwidth tests (simulated 1.5 Mbps hotel Wi-Fi), Zoom maintained usable video when
competitors dropped to audio-only. Its noise suppression — now AI-powered on all plans —
removed keyboard clatter and HVAC hum more effectively than any competitor.

The free tier remains the most generous for casual use: unlimited 1:1 meetings and 40-minute
group calls with up to 100 participants. The Pro plan at $13.33/month per host (billed
annually) removes the time limit, adds 5GB cloud recording, and includes the AI Companion —
meeting summaries, smart chapters, and real-time translation across 36 languages.

The Business plan at $18.33/month per host (10-host minimum) adds managed domains, company
branding, and an admin dashboard. For large events, Zoom Webinars start at $79/month for
500 attendees.

The AI Companion is the differentiator in 2026. Unlike competitors that charge extra for AI
features, Zoom includes it on every paid plan. In our transcript accuracy tests, it scored
94.1% on clear audio and 87.3% with background noise — the best of the four platforms.

## Best value for Google shops: Google Meet

Google Meet's advantage is not the product itself — it is the **zero marginal cost** for the
millions of teams already paying for Google Workspace. Meet is included in every Workspace
plan, starting at $7/user/month (Business Starter). If your team lives in Gmail, Calendar,
and Drive, adding a separate video tool is pure waste.

The standalone free tier allows 60-minute group calls with up to 100 participants — more
generous than Zoom's 40-minute cap. Paid Workspace tiers add recording (Business Standard
at $14/user/month), noise cancellation, attendance reports, and live streaming to YouTube.

In our quality tests, Meet performed well on good connections but degraded faster than Zoom
under bandwidth pressure. Its AI features — "Take notes for me" and meeting summaries via
Gemini — require the Gemini Business add-on ($20/user/month), which is a significant
upcharge compared to Zoom's included AI Companion.

## Best for Microsoft enterprises: Microsoft Teams

Microsoft Teams is less a video conferencing tool and more a **collaboration operating
system**. Video calls are one feature among persistent chat, file co-editing, Planner,
SharePoint integration, and now Copilot agents. For organizations standardized on Microsoft
365, Teams is the path of least resistance.

The free tier supports 60-minute group calls with 100 participants. Teams Essentials at
$4/user/month removes the time limit and adds 10GB cloud storage. But the real value is in
Microsoft 365 Business Basic at $6/user/month, which bundles Teams with Exchange, SharePoint,
and the web versions of Office apps.

In our testing, Teams had the highest resource consumption — the desktop app used 1.2GB of
RAM during a 1-hour meeting, compared to Zoom's 480MB. Video quality was solid but not
best-in-class, and guest joining requires more clicks than Zoom or Meet. Where Teams
excels: meeting intelligence via Copilot ($30/user/month add-on), which produces the most
structured meeting recaps of any platform.

## The enterprise veteran: Webex

Webex by Cisco is the oldest platform in this comparison, and it shows — in both directions.
The interface feels a generation behind, but the enterprise features are genuinely deep:
end-to-end encryption by default, FedRAMP High authorization, and the strongest compliance
portfolio of the four platforms.

The free tier allows 40-minute group meetings with 100 participants. The Meet plan at
$14.50/month per host removes the time limit and adds 10GB recording. The Suite plan at
$25/user/month adds calling, messaging, and polling.

In our quality tests, Webex delivered solid video but the highest latency — averaging 340ms
round-trip compared to Zoom's 210ms. Its AI Assistant (included on paid plans) produces
decent summaries but trails Zoom's AI Companion in transcript accuracy.

## Pricing value analysis

Cost comparison for a 10-person team where everyone needs to host meetings:

| Platform | Plan | Monthly cost (10 hosts) | What you get |
|----------|------|------------------------|--------------|
| Google Meet | Workspace Starter | $70 (entire team) | Meet + Gmail + Drive + Docs |
| Microsoft Teams | M365 Business Basic | $60 (entire team) | Teams + Exchange + SharePoint + Office web |
| Zoom | Pro | $133 (10 hosts) | Zoom only |
| Webex | Meet | $145 (10 hosts) | Webex only |

*Prices verified July 2026, annual billing. Google and Microsoft pricing is per-user for the
entire suite; Zoom and Webex are per-host for the video product only.*

The math strongly favors the suites — but only if you are not already paying for a
competing ecosystem. A Google Workspace team adding Zoom pays $133/month on top of $70.
A standalone organization choosing purely on meeting quality pays more for Zoom, but gets
the best product.

## Who should use what?

**Choose Zoom if:** Meeting quality is your top priority, you frequently host external
clients or partners, or you want AI meeting features without paying extra. The best
standalone video product, period.

**Choose Google Meet if:** Your team already uses Google Workspace. It is included, it is
good enough for daily standups and client calls, and adding another tool just creates
license sprawl.

**Choose Microsoft Teams if:** Your organization is standardized on Microsoft 365 and you
need persistent collaboration (chat, files, projects) alongside video. The compliance
tooling is also best-in-class for regulated industries.

**Choose Webex if:** You are in a highly regulated industry (government, defense,
healthcare) where FedRAMP High and default end-to-end encryption are requirements, or you
need Cisco's conference room hardware ecosystem.

**Our overall pick: Zoom Workplace.** When a meeting matters — client pitches, board calls,

included rather than an add-on seals the value argument.

is free money. It handles daily meetings competently and the 60-minute free tier is the most
generous.

**For Microsoft shops: Teams.** Do not fight your ecosystem. Teams is included, integrated,
and good enough — and Copilot's meeting recaps are worth the add-on for meeting-heavy
organizations.
