Project management software has a dirty secret: most teams use about 15% of the features they pay
for. The other 85% sits behind enterprise paywalls, requires a consultant to configure, or simply
duplicates what a spreadsheet already does.

This comparison tests five platforms — **ClickUp, Linear, Monday.com, Asana, and Notion** — against
the same three workflows: planning a two-week sprint with dependencies, coordinating a cross-team
product launch, and generating a client-facing progress report.

## How we tested

Same team, same projects, same deadlines. We ran a five-person team through identical scenarios in
each tool: a two-week sprint with 23 tasks and 6 dependencies, a cross-team product launch
involving 3 departments, and a client report that needed to look presentable without a designer.

We measured:

1. **Setup friction** — how long from signup to first usable board?
2. **Dependency handling** — did blocked tasks actually block, or just show a warning?
3. **Reporting quality** — could we generate a client-ready report without exporting to PowerPoint?
4. **AI usefulness** — did the AI features save time or just add noise?
5. **Price honesty** — does the advertised price match what you actually pay for a functional setup?

## The winner: ClickUp

ClickUp wins on a simple metric: features per dollar. At $7/month per user on annual billing (the
Unlimited plan), you get unlimited storage, unlimited integrations, native time tracking, goals,
portfolio management, and guest access with permission controls. No other tool in this comparison
comes close to that feature density at that price point.

The free plan is also the most generous here — unlimited tasks, unlimited members, kanban boards,
sprint management, and calendar view. The 60MB storage cap is the main limitation, but for teams
that primarily manage tasks rather than files, it is genuinely usable.

The AI pricing is where ClickUp gets interesting. Instead of bundling AI into every plan (and
charging everyone for it), they offer it as a separate add-on: Brain AI at $9/mo per user for
assistant and chat features, or Everything AI at $28/mo for the full agentic suite including
notetaker, image generation, and AI automations. You can also buy AI Super Credits at $0.001 each
($10 per 10,000 credits) for pay-as-you-go agent usage.

## Best for engineering teams: Linear

Linear is what happens when a project management tool is designed by engineers, for engineers. At
$10/month per user (Basic, billed yearly), you get unlimited issues, 5 teams, unlimited file
uploads, and admin roles. The Business plan at $16/mo adds unlimited teams, private teams, Triage
Intelligence, and Linear Agent automations.

The speed difference is not marketing. Linear's interface is built on a local-first architecture
that makes every interaction feel instant. Creating an issue, changing a status, reordering a
sprint — everything happens in under 50ms. In a tool you touch 50+ times a day, that adds up.

The free plan is limited (2 teams, 250 issues) but includes the agent platform and Linear Agent,
making it a genuine option for small side projects.

## Best for non-technical teams: Monday.com

Monday.com is the project management tool your marketing team will actually use without complaining.
The interface is visual, colorful, and requires zero training to understand. At $9/seat/month
(Basic, billed annually), you get unlimited items, unlimited free viewers, and 1,000 AI credits.

The AI credit system is Monday's differentiator in 2026. Every plan includes AI credits that power
agents, the Sidekick assistant, a meeting notetaker, and a "vibe app builder" that turns plain
language descriptions into working business apps. Standard ($12/mo) gets 2,000 credits, Pro
($19/mo) gets 3,000, and Enterprise gets custom allocations.

The catch: Monday's pricing page shows four separate products (Work Management, CRM, Service, Dev)
with different price points. The $9/mo price is for Work Management only. If you need CRM features,
that starts at $12/mo. Service starts at $31/mo. This à la carte approach can make budgeting
confusing.

## The structured PMO choice: Asana

Asana is the project management tool that enterprise PMOs standardize on. At $10.99/mo per user
(Starter, billed annually), you get unlimited tasks, timeline and Gantt views, reporting
dashboards, unlimited automations, and AI Studio Basic with 50K credits. The Advanced plan at
$24.99/mo adds portfolios, goals, workload management, and time tracking.

The free plan (Personal) supports up to 2 users with unlimited tasks and projects — genuinely
useful for a freelancer or a two-person team. But the jump from free to Starter is significant:
you go from list/board/calendar views to timeline, Gantt, and dashboards.

Asana's AI strategy in 2026 centers on "AI Teammates" — ready-to-go AI agents that work like team
members. They are available as an add-on on Starter and above. The AI Studio (included on paid
plans) is a no-code builder for creating custom AI workflows.

## The docs-first wildcard: Notion

Notion is not really a project management tool. It is a workspace that happens to include databases,
kanban boards, and timeline views. At $10/mo per member (Plus, billed yearly), you get unlimited
collaborative blocks, unlimited file uploads, custom forms, and basic connections. The Business
plan at $20/mo adds the Notion Agent, AI meeting notes, enterprise search, and SAML SSO.

The free plan is unlimited for individuals but limited for teams (block storage cap for 2+ members).
Students and educators get Plus free with a school email.

Where Notion shines is the intersection of documentation and project management. If your team needs
a wiki, a knowledge base, AND a task tracker in one tool, Notion eliminates the need for Confluence
+ Jira. The new Custom Agents ($10 per 1,000 credits) and Workers (beta) extend this further.

Where it falls short: dependency management is basic, there is no native time tracking, and
reporting requires building custom database views. For pure project management, it is a compromise.

## Pricing deep dive: what you actually pay

The advertised prices tell only part of the story. Here is what a 10-person team actually pays for
a functional setup on annual billing:

| Tool | Base plan | Per-user cost | 10-person monthly | AI add-on | Total with AI |
|------|-----------|--------------|-------------------|-----------|---------------|
| ClickUp | Unlimited | $7/mo | $70 | Brain AI $9/mo | $160 |
| Linear | Basic | $10/mo | $100 | Included (basic) | $100 |
| Monday.com | Basic | $9/mo | $90 | Included (1K credits) | $90 |
| Asana | Starter | $10.99/mo | $109.90 | AI Studio included | $109.90 |
| Notion | Plus | $10/mo | $100 | Trial only | $100 |

**Key observations:**

- **ClickUp is cheapest without AI, but the AI add-on ($9/mo) nearly doubles the cost.** If you
  need the full agentic suite (Everything AI at $28/mo), a 10-person team pays $350/month — more
  than any other tool.
- **Linear includes basic AI (Agent, coding sessions) from the free plan.** The Business plan
  ($16/mo) adds automations and code intelligence without a separate AI surcharge.
- **Monday.com bundles AI credits into every paid plan.** The 1,000 credits on Basic may not last
  long with heavy agent usage, but you are not paying a separate AI subscription.
- **Asana includes AI Studio on all paid plans** (50K credits on Starter, 75K on Advanced, 200K
  on Enterprise). AI Teammates are a separate add-on.
- **Notion's AI is still in trial on Plus and Business.** Custom Agents cost $10 per 1,000 credits
  on top of your subscription.

## The AI question: is it worth paying for?

Every PM tool in 2026 has an AI story. Here is the honest assessment after two weeks of testing:

**Actually useful:**
- **Linear Agent** for auto-triaging issues and suggesting assignees
- **Monday Sidekick** for natural-language board queries ("show me all overdue tasks assigned to
  Sarah")
- **Asana Smart Status** for auto-generating project status updates
- **ClickUp Brain** for summarizing long task threads

**Still gimmicky:**
- AI-generated project plans (they ignore your actual constraints)
- "Vibe app builders" that create apps you will never use
- AI meeting notetakers that produce summaries nobody reads

**The bottom line:** AI features are a tiebreaker, not a decision driver. Choose the tool that fits
your team's workflow first. If two tools are otherwise equal, the one with better AI wins.

## FAQ

### Which project management tool has the best free plan?

ClickUp, by a wide margin. Unlimited tasks, unlimited members, kanban boards, sprint management,
and calendar view — all free. The only real limitation is 60MB of storage. Notion's free plan is
unlimited for individuals but capped for teams. Asana's free plan supports only 2 users.

### Can I switch from Jira to one of these tools?

Yes. All five tools offer Jira importers. Linear has the most mature Jira migration path and is
the most common destination for engineering teams leaving Jira. ClickUp and Monday.com also handle
Jira imports well. Expect 1-2 weeks of adjustment regardless of which tool you choose.

### Which tool is best for client-facing work?

Monday.com and Asana. Both have strong reporting dashboards, guest access for external
stakeholders, and client-ready views. ClickUp also supports guest access with permission controls.
Linear is not designed for client-facing workflows.

### Do I need to pay for AI features separately?

It depends on the tool. Linear includes basic AI from the free plan. Monday.com and Asana bundle
AI credits into paid plans. ClickUp and Notion charge separately for AI. If AI features are
important to you, factor the add-on cost into your total budget.

### What is the cheapest option for a 5-person startup?

ClickUp Unlimited at $7/mo per user = $35/month for 5 people. You get unlimited storage, time
tracking, goals, and integrations. Linear Basic at $10/mo = $50/month is the runner-up if you are
an engineering team. Both are excellent choices.
